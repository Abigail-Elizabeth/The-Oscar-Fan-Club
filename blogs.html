Northern Lights

On 11th October 2024, the northern lights were visible across the UK and Ireland! Determined to see the lights in all their glory, we trekked (walked) all the way up Patrick’s hill, and set up camp (two jackets on the ground). Everyone must’ve had the same idea because it was pretty packed for a random spot at 10 pm. We set up our cameras for those beautiful long exposure shots, and opened up the snacks as we waited. Disappointed was an understatement. Not one single speck of light presented itself, and in true Irish fashion, the clouds mocked us from above. We took the last bus back, and were greeted by the same bus driver who took us in. “Didn’t I pick you girls up earlier?”. Apparently we were loud enough to leave an impression.

Fort Camden

On 9th November 2024, we were supposed to go on the Ballycotton cliff walk. However, due to unprecedented circumstances (everyone’s general lack of desire to catch a bus at 7 am), we took the 220 straight to Camden! Well, almost. We missed the bus by about 15 seconds (you should’ve seen Ruthie run), so we walked into town and got breakfast before catching the next bus to Camden. It was a gloomy drizzly day, but there isn’t much that can take away from the simple joy of seaside air. Immediately upon arrival we noticed that the little town had tons of semi-abandoned traffic cones. Maïlys, who had been insisting that we get a traffic cone to dress up as a Christmas tree, reiterated this very important need of the household. We did not let her steal any traffic cones. We walked along the Crosshaven cliff loop, and much to our delight, the tide was out so the beach was open! Shoes were kicked off, trousers rolled up, and we attempted to frolic in the 9 degree water. Of course, that got old pretty fast, so we sat on the rocks and had our lunch. After two hours on the beach, we made our way back to the bus stop, at which point Marie managed to pull off an insanely efficient Cathedral visit, and Ruthie got food from a fish and chips shop (shout out to Chish N Fips, Crosshaven). The day concluded as we told Carol (who stayed back on account of a pesky assignment) about our day and emptied our pockets of rocks and shells from the beach.

Engine

The Engine Project was probably the event of the month of November. Carol was supposed to build a functional Beta Stirling Engine for her thermodynamics class. The preparation was immense - soda cans were bought, chugged, and cleaned every day, balloons filled, candles and lighters restocked. She spent everyday at the kitchen table, making her way through the massive 62 step protocol, trying to explain the engine to us laymen. Eventually, Ruthie and Maïlys got involved, and the three of them worked very hard on the infernal thing. Ruthie’s need for perfection translated well into the measurement and step-following, and Maïlys’ stellar personality and saintly patience made for wonderful emotional support. Everyone stayed up till 2 am on most days, lighting, blowing out, and relighting candles. Cleaning wax up from the floor. Trying not to inhale the dust from cutting up soda cans or steel wool. In the end, they managed to get the engine running for all of 4 seconds. It was a rather underwhelming and unjust conclusion to the herculean effort that was put in. But on the bright side, Carol turned it in, and her professor assured them that it doesn’t always work. Of course, that made nothing better, but at least she’ll get the A for effort!

Dumplings

Following a Crazy Rich Asians movie night, we found ourselves craving dumplings. However, nobody felt like spending 7 euro per 10 dumplings from the store, so we got the ingredients and got to work. Abi made the dough, and Marie whipped up two fillings - one vegetarian and one pork. We sat around the table and started making the dumplings in an assembly line. Carol taught us how to fold them, but clearly she was a master Dumpling Folder, and none of us could get it right. They were extremely funny looking, and there was zero uniformity. We then cooked them three different ways. Marie manned the airfryer, Maïlys pan-fried, Abi watched the steamer, and Ruthie made the sauces. And after everyone’s patience ran out, we started eating them as and when they were done cooking. They were extremely delicious. Or maybe we were just hungry. Either way, we got over 150 dumplings for under 10 euros, so this was an absolute win for us. #SustainableChineseFood

Galway

On 2nd December 2024, we went to Galway to see the Christmas market! We watched the sun rise in Cork as we waited for the bus, and were soon on our way. Three hours later, the sun was high in the sky (well, about as high as it can get in December) and it was a gorgeous day in Galway. The market wasn’t opening till 12, so we made our way to the nearest pub to have some breakfast. We spent most of the afternoon roaming around the city centre, popping in and out of all the stores. Ruthie found a book she’d been looking for at a small family bookshop, Abi got some neat hiking shoes (finally!), and Marie went nuts over every claddagh ring shop we passed. We went to every clothing store we saw, trying on all the pretty gowns and funky headdresses. We saw the sunset at Mutton Island. Or at least, that was the plan. Unfortunately, the clouds were back on their mission to thwart our every attempt to experience a light phenomena. We headed back to the Christmas market, and the whole place was alive! There were lights everywhere, painted decorations, and vendors with a whole assortment of knick-knacks. Abi got herself a little beanie hat that she wears almost every day now. We got on the ferris wheel and Ruthie had to face the fact that she might be slightly not okay with heights. Finally, we headed into a pub (with live music!) for a quick dinner before speed-walking back to the bus, and heading home to Cork.
